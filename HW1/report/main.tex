\section{Introduction}

This report covers the analysis of a single-phase variable reluctance
machine (VRM) consisting of a rectangular laminated-steel stator and a
D-shaped rotor. As the rotor turns, the air-gap reluctance changes,
producing an electromagnetic torque. The work is divided into four
parts: analytical modelling, FEA with linear materials, FEA with
non-linear materials, and a proposed control strategy for continuous
rotation.

\subsection{Assumptions}

To keep the analytical model tractable, the following assumptions are made:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\alph*)]
  \item The core is \textbf{infinitely permeable} ($\mu_r \to \infty$),
        so only the air-gap reluctance matters.
  \item There is \textbf{no fringing flux} -- the effective flux area
        equals the geometric core cross-section.
  \item The \textbf{inductance varies sinusoidally} with twice the
        mechanical angle, which is a standard approximation for a 2-pole
        machine.
  \item The system is \textbf{lossless} -- no eddy-current or hysteresis
        effects.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Core Geometry}

\textbf{Core cross-sectional area:}
\begin{equation}
  A_{\mathrm{core}} = w_c \times d = 15 \times 25 = 375\unit{mm^2}
  = 375 \times 10^{-6}\unit{m^2}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Mean magnetic path length} (centre-line of the stator):
\begin{align}
  \ell_{\mathrm{core}}
    &= 2(w_w + w_c) + 2(h_w + w_c) \notag \\
    &= 2(30+15) + 2(40+15) = 200\unit{mm} = 0.2\unit{m}
\end{align}

With the infinite-permeability assumption the core reluctance is zero,
so $\ell_{\mathrm{core}}$ plays no role in the inductance calculation
(it becomes relevant when finite permeability or saturation is
included in Section~\ref{sec:improve}).

\textbf{Air-gap lengths.} The flux crosses two gaps in series:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Aligned} ($\theta = 0^{\circ}$): both gaps equal the
        design clearance:
        \[
          \ell_{g,\min} = 2g = 2 \times 0.5 = 1\unit{mm}
        \]
  \item \emph{Unaligned} ($\theta = 90^{\circ}$): the rotor flat
        faces the pole tips, increasing each gap to $2.5\unit{mm}$:
        \[
          \ell_{g,\max} = 2 \times 2.5 = 5\unit{mm}
        \]
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Maximum and Minimum Inductance}

With an infinitely permeable core the reluctance is purely due to
the air gap:
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{R} = \frac{\ell_g}{\mu_0 A_{\mathrm{core}}}
  \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad
  L = \frac{N^2}{\mathcal{R}} = \frac{N^2 \mu_0 A_{\mathrm{core}}}{\ell_g}
\end{equation}

\textbf{At the aligned position} ($\ell_g = \ell_{g,\min} = 1\unit{mm}$):
\begin{align}
  \mathcal{R}_{\min}
    &= \frac{1\times10^{-3}}{4\pi\times10^{-7}\times375\times10^{-6}}
     = 2.122\times10^{6}\unit{A/Wb} \\
  L_{\max}
    &= \frac{300^2}{2.122\times10^{6}} = 42.41\unit{mH}
\end{align}

\textbf{At the unaligned position} ($\ell_g = \ell_{g,\max} = 5\unit{mm}$):
\begin{align}
  \mathcal{R}_{\max}
    &= \frac{5\times10^{-3}}{4\pi\times10^{-7}\times375\times10^{-6}}
     = 10.61\times10^{6}\unit{A/Wb} \\
  L_{\min}
    &= \frac{300^2}{10.61\times10^{6}} = 8.48\unit{mH}
\end{align}

The results are summarised in Table~\ref{tab:LR}.

  \label{tab:LR}
  \renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}

\subsection{Inductance Variation $L(\theta)$}

Assuming a sinusoidal variation between $L_{\min}$ and $L_{\max}$:

\begin{equation}
  \boxed{L(\theta) = \frac{L_{\max}+L_{\min}}{2}
    + \frac{L_{\max}-L_{\min}}{2}\cos(2\theta)}
  \label{eq:Ltheta}
\end{equation}

This satisfies $L(0^{\circ}) = L_{\max}$ and $L(90^{\circ}) = L_{\min}$.
The factor $2\theta$ reflects the 2-pole symmetry: one full inductance
cycle every $180^{\circ}$ of mechanical rotation.

\subsection{Torque $T(\theta)$}

For constant current excitation the torque is the co-energy derivative:
\begin{equation}
  T(\theta) = \frac{1}{2}\,i^2\,\frac{dL}{d\theta}
  \label{eq:Tcoenergy}
\end{equation}

Differentiating Equation~(\ref{eq:Ltheta}):
\begin{equation}
  \frac{dL}{d\theta} = -(L_{\max}-L_{\min})\sin(2\theta)
\end{equation}

So the torque is:
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{T(\theta) = -\frac{i^2}{2}(L_{\max}-L_{\min})\sin(2\theta)}
  \label{eq:Ttheta}
\end{equation}

The peak torque amplitude at $I = 2.5\unit{A}$:
\begin{align}
  T_{\mathrm{peak}}
    &= \frac{I^2}{2}(L_{\max}-L_{\min}) \notag \\
    &= \frac{(2.5)^2}{2}\times(42.41-8.48)\times10^{-3}
     = 106.0\unit{mN\cdot m}
  \label{eq:Tpeak}
\end{align}

The average torque under constant DC is zero (positive and negative
halves cancel). The inductance and torque plots are shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:LT}.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{../analytical_LT_plots.png}
  \caption{Inductance (top) and torque (bottom) vs.\ rotor angle,
           from the analytical model. $L$ ranges from $8.48\unit{mH}$
           to $42.41\unit{mH}$; peak torque is $\pm106\unit{mN\cdot m}$.}
  \label{fig:LT}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Improving the Model}
\label{sec:improve}

The main limitations of the model above and how to address them:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Finite core permeability.} Including the core
        reluctance $\mathcal{R}_{\mathrm{core}} =
        \ell_{\mathrm{core}}/(\mu_0\mu_r A_c)$ in series with the
        air-gap reluctance reduces $L_{\max}$ and changes the
        $L_{\max}/L_{\min}$ ratio.

  \item \textbf{Magnetic saturation.} At high currents $\mu_r$ drops
        and $L$ becomes a function of both $\theta$ and $i$.
        The torque must then be computed from the full co-energy
        integral $T = \partial W'(\theta,i)/\partial\theta$.

  \item \textbf{Fringing flux.} The effective flux area at the gap
        edges is larger than $A_{\mathrm{core}}$.  A simple correction
        replaces $w_c$ and $d$ with $(w_c+\ell_g)$ and $(d+\ell_g)$,
        which increases both $L_{\max}$ and $L_{\min}$ (more so at the
        larger gap).

  \item \textbf{Non-sinusoidal profile.} The actual inductance
        variation follows the change in overlapping pole-arc area with
        angle and is closer to a trapezoidal shape than a sinusoid.
        
\end{enumerate}

\section{Q2 -- FEA Modelling (Linear Materials)}

\subsection{Model Setup}

The machine was modelled in ANSYS Maxwell 2D (Student Edition 2025~R2)
using a Magnetostatic solver.  The stator was drawn as a
$60\times70\unit{mm}$ rectangle with the $30\times40\unit{mm}$ window
and $13\unit{mm}$ bore subtracted.  The D-shaped rotor (pole radius
$12.5\unit{mm}$, notch radius $12\unit{mm}$) was placed at the bore
centre.  Two $10\times30\unit{mm}$ coil cross-sections were added on the
left leg.

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Excitation:} Winding of 300~turns, $I = 2.5\unit{A}$ DC.
        Coil terminals added with opposite polarities.
  \item \textbf{Boundary:} Large surrounding rectangular region with magnetic potential set as zero.
  \item \textbf{Mesh:} $0.25\unit{mm}$ maximum element in the air gap;

  \item \textbf{Material:} Linear iron with constant relative permeability
        $\mu_r = 4000$.
  \item \textbf{Sweep:} Rotor angle from $0^{\circ}$ to $360^{\circ}$ in

\textbf{Fringing flux} is the dominant source of discrepancy in
inductance.  At the unaligned position the total air gap is
$5\unit{mm}$, which is comparable to the core width ($15\unit{mm}$);
fringing fields extend well beyond the pole face and significantly
increase the effective flux area.  This raises $L_{\min}$ by roughly a
factor of two compared to the analytical result.  At the aligned
position (gap $= 1\unit{mm}$), fringing is less severe, giving only a
$25\%$ increase in $L_{\max}$.  Since the material is linear, there is
no saturation; the high $\mu_r = 4000$ means the core reluctance is
negligible and the infinite-permeability assumption of the analytical
model is well justified.

\textbf{Torque peak} is essentially unchanged ($-1\%$) despite the
large inductance differences.  Because torque depends on $dL/d\theta$,
and fringing increases both $L_{\max}$ and $L_{\min}$ by similar
absolute amounts, the slope of $L(\theta)$ and hence the peak torque
remain nearly identical to the analytical prediction.

\textbf{Torque asymmetry and shifted zero crossings.}
The FEA torque curve is noticeably asymmetric about $90^{\circ}$ and
$180^{\circ}$, whereas the analytical model is a perfect sinusoid.
Two physical reasons explain this:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \emph{Non-symmetric stator geometry.} The rotor centre is at
        $(45,\,35)\unit{mm}$, offset $15\unit{mm}$ from the stator
        centre $(30,\,35)\unit{mm}$.  The right yoke is only
        $\sim 2\unit{mm}$ thick while the left leg is $15\unit{mm}$,
        so the magnetic circuit has a different reluctance profile for
        clockwise and counter-clockwise rotor excursions.

  \item \emph{Non-sinusoidal inductance profile.} The D-shaped rotor
        notch creates a flat plateau in $L(\theta)$ near the unaligned
        position ($L \approx 17.1\unit{mH}$ from $85^{\circ}$ to
        $95^{\circ}$).  Since $T = \tfrac{1}{2}I^2\,dL/d\theta$, a flat
        region gives near-zero torque over a wider angular range than a
        sinusoid predicts, and the slopes on either side are unequal.
\end{enumerate}

These effects are inherent to the actual geometry and are correctly
captured by FEA but not by the sinusoidal analytical model.

\textbf{Saturation reduces $L_{\max}$ significantly.}
At the aligned position the total air gap is only $1\unit{mm}$, so most
of the MMF ($750\unit{A\text{-}turns}$) drives flux through the core.
With steel\_1010 the operating flux density reaches approximately
$0.8\unit{T}$ in the pole legs; at this level the relative permeability
of steel\_1010 is around $\mu_r \approx 700\text{--}900$, far below the
$\mu_r = 4000$ assumed in Q2.  The effective core reluctance therefore
rises, reducing $L_{\max}$ from $53.14\unit{mH}$ (linear FEA) to
$44.24\unit{mH}$ (nonlinear FEA), a $16.8\%$ decrease.

\textbf{$L_{\min}$ is nearly unchanged.}
At the unaligned position the large air gap ($5\unit{mm}$) dominates
the reluctance.  The core carries only $B \approx 0.18\unit{T}$, well
within the linear region of the steel\_1010 curve (see
Appendix~\ref{app:bh}), so saturation has negligible effect.
$L_{\min}$ falls only from $17.07\unit{mH}$ to $16.19\unit{mH}$
($-5\%$).

\textbf{Peak torque is reduced by $\approx 25\%$.}
Because $T_{\mathrm{peak}} = \tfrac{1}{2}I^{2}(L_{\max}-L_{\min})$,
the drop in $L_{\max}$ with little change in $L_{\min}$ reduces
$(L_{\max}-L_{\min})$ from $36.1\unit{mH}$ to $28.1\unit{mH}$, giving
a peak torque of $78.1\unit{mN\cdot m}$ compared to
$104.9\unit{mN\cdot m}$ for the linear model.

\textbf{Fringing vs.\ saturation.}
Both effects are present.  Fringing increases $L_{\max}$ above the
analytical value (gap is small, fringing adds area), while saturation
pushes $L_{\max}$ back down.  In this design the two effects partially
cancel, leaving the nonlinear $L_{\max}$ ($44.24\unit{mH}$) only about
$4\%$ above the analytical prediction ($42.41\unit{mH}$).  For
$L_{\min}$, fringing dominates (gap is large, $\mu_r$ is high anyway),
so the nonlinear and linear FEA values are nearly identical and both are
roughly twice the analytical result.

\section{Q4 -- Control Method for Continuous Rotation}
\label{sec:control}

\subsection{The Problem}

With constant DC current the torque averages to zero over a full
rotation. For net unidirectional motion the current must be switched
on only when it produces positive torque.

\subsection{Switched Excitation}

From Equation~(\ref{eq:Ttheta}), torque is positive when
$\sin(2\theta) < 0$, i.e.\ when
$\theta \in (90^{\circ}, 180^{\circ}) \cup (270^{\circ}, 360^{\circ})$.
The strategy is simple: \textbf{energise the coil as the rotor moves
from unaligned toward aligned; switch off before the aligned position
to avoid negative torque on the return stroke.}

\subsection{Average Torque}

With ideal on/off switching (on at $90^{\circ}$, off at $180^{\circ}$):
\begin{align}
  T_{\mathrm{avg}}
    &= \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{90^{\circ}}^{180^{\circ}}
       \frac{I^2}{2}(L_{\max}-L_{\min})(-\sin 2\theta)\,d\theta
     = \frac{T_{\mathrm{peak}}}{\pi}
\end{align}

For the analytical model ($T_{\mathrm{peak}} = 106.0\unit{mN\cdot m}$):
$T_{\mathrm{avg}} \approx 33.8\unit{mN\cdot m}$ ($31.8\%$ of peak).
For the nonlinear FEA ($T_{\mathrm{peak}} = 78.1\unit{mN\cdot m}$):
$T_{\mathrm{avg}} \approx 24.9\unit{mN\cdot m}$ ($31.8\%$ of peak, same ratio).

Figure~\ref{fig:switched} shows the switched torque waveform for all
three models.

  \label{fig:switched}

\subsection{Practical Considerations}

\begin{itemize}
  \item A position sensor (Hall effect or encoder) is needed to
        synchronise switching with rotor angle.
  \item The turn-on angle should be slightly before $90^{\circ}$ and
        the turn-off angle slightly before $180^{\circ}$ to compensate
        for the finite current rise and fall times.
  \item A PWM current controller keeps the current constant during the
        on-interval for repeatable torque.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}

The analytical model predicts $L_{\min} = 8.48\unit{mH}$,
$L_{\max} = 42.41\unit{mH}$, and a peak torque of
$106\unit{mN\cdot m}$ under $2.5\unit{A}$ DC.  Linear FEA with iron
($\mu_r = 4000$) yields $L_{\min} = 17.07\unit{mH}$ and
$L_{\max} = 53.14\unit{mH}$, primarily because fringing flux at the
large unaligned gap is not captured by the analytical model.  Despite
the large inductance discrepancy, the FEA peak torque
($104.9\unit{mN\cdot m}$) matches the analytical value to within $1\%$,
confirming that the sinusoidal torque expression is adequate for
engineering purposes.  Nonlinear FEA (Q3) with steel\_1010 reduces $L_{\max}$ to
$44.24\unit{mH}$ due to core saturation at $B \approx 0.8\unit{T}$,
while $L_{\min}$ is nearly unchanged ($16.19\unit{mH}$), lowering the
peak torque to $78.1\unit{mN\cdot m}$.  Switching the excitation on
during the positive-torque stroke raises the average torque to
$33.8\unit{mN\cdot m}$ (analytical) and $24.9\unit{mN\cdot m}$
(nonlinear FEA).
